Build Patterns

The confidence problem, measured — and why it's a design brief for every advice UI.

Study: AI advice makes people 3× less accurate and 2× more confidentarxiv / theregister.com · 19 Jul

A new paper (Capraro, Milano-Bicocca; Marcoccia, ENS; Quattrociocchi, Sapienza) had people answer questions where the AI is usually wrong. Giving them access to AI advice collapsed "I don't know" from 44% → 3%, dropped accuracy 27% → 9%, and raised confidence 30% → 76%. Paying for correct answers barely moved it — "I don't know" rose only to 8%, accuracy to 16%. The title says it plainly: AI advice "suppresses people's willingness to say 'I don't know', even when the advice is wrong and accuracy is incentivized."

For an advice product — financial advice most of all — this is your core UX risk, quantified: the interface mints confidence faster than it mints correctness, and money doesn't fix it. Design against the grain: surface calibrated uncertainty, make "not sure — get a human" a first-class output rather than a failure state, add friction on high-stakes answers, and cite sources so the user verifies instead of absorbs. An advice UI that never says "I don't know" isn't confident — it's a liability engine, and now there's a number on it.

Your Radar

A quiet cost cliff in Codex, and an open-weight claim to keep half an eye on.

Dated cost signal: Codex caps GPT-5.6 context at 272K, 2× pricing above itgithub / HN · 19 Jul

OpenAI reversed the bump to 372K — Codex now caps GPT-5.6 (Sol/Terra/Luna) at 272K tokens. The reason is billing: requests over 272K input are charged 2× input / 1.5× output for the whole request, and Codex won't let you opt into the longer advertised window at all. The catch for builders: many third-party harnesses still hardcode 372K, silently burning quota at the higher rate.

If you or your tooling pin GPT-5.6 to a 372K window, you may be paying 2× and hitting hard caps mid-run without knowing why. Audit your harness config today and budget context against the 272K economic cliff, not the "1.05M" on the spec sheet. Same lesson as everything else this week — the number that matters is the one you can actually verify against your bill.
