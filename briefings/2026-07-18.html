The bottleneck was never the code

Three of today's sharpest posts share one question: where is the real constraint? The New Stack argues that AI didn't move the software bottleneck from writing code to reviewing it — 85% of teams say it did, but if merged pull requests pile up undeployed, the constraint was always downstream of review, in the release pipeline. Two arguments about mislocated bottlenecks — one in your delivery pipeline, one in how models are trained.

The New Stack: AI hasn't shifted the bottleneck from coding to code review — the constraint is downstreamthenewstack.io · 17 Jul

The essay pushes back on the consensus that review is the new chokepoint. The data it cites is real: GitLab's 2026 survey found 85% of teams believe AI moved the bottleneck to review; Faros AI's study of 10,000 developers found high-AI teams merge 98% more PRs while review time on those changes climbs 91%. The counter is a theory-of-constraints read: if reviewed-and-merged code keeps accumulating without reaching users, then review isn't the binding constraint — deployment and release are. Adding reviewers or review-bots to a non-bottleneck just moves the pile.

Before you buy an AI code-review tool or throw senior engineers at review, find where work-in-progress actually accumulates. If PRs merge fast but sit behind a slow release train, batched deploys, or a manual QA gate, that's your constraint — and speeding up the steps ahead of it makes the pile bigger, not the product faster. For a small wealthtech team shipping AI-written code daily, the leverage is usually continuous deploy and feature-flagged release, not more eyes on the diff.

Sean Goedecke: overtraining as the path to human-like AIseangoedecke.com · 17 Jul

Goedecke unpacks Gwern's counterintuitive proposal: instead of moderately-sized models on internet-scale data, train enormous models (he floats 100T parameters) on small datasets. The bet rests on grokking — the observation that a model trained on constrained data eventually stops memorizing and discovers the underlying rule. His line: "if you feed the model all the data in the world, it can continue to improve simply by memorizing more." Scarcity forces the harder, more general solution. He's clear it's speculative and unproven at scale.

A useful lens for the make-vs-rent question, even before the theory is settled: some tasks want recall (retrieve the exact clause, the exact rate) and some want reasoning (generalize across a client's situation). When you evaluate a base model — or decide whether to fine-tune one on your domain — ask which of those you're actually buying. A model that has grokked a domain's structure beats one that has merely memorized more of its documents.

Build Patterns

Evals as diagnosis, not scoreboard — one for multimodal perception, one for the writing your model ships.

Kimi's PerceptionBench: isolate seeing from reasoning, and no model clears 60%kimi.com · 17 Jul

Moonshot built PerceptionBench by studying how frontier models fail across 40+ existing visual benchmarks, then distilling 10 atomic perception skills — counting, depth, localization, OCR, fine-grained recognition, hallucination and more — into 3,000 questions answerable by looking alone, no reasoning or outside knowledge. Findings are pointed: no model reached 60%; models with similar overall scores have wildly different perceptual profiles; and many correct answers don't replicate on retry, implying models guess rather than perceive.

If your product reads documents, statements, IDs or charts, this is the eval design to copy: test perception separately from reasoning, because a model that reasons well over a misread figure fails silently and confidently. The non-replication finding is the real warning — run perception-critical prompts more than once and treat unstable answers as a red flag, not noise. For financial documents where a transposed digit is a real-money error, a separate perception gate belongs in your eval suite.

A small tool, but a clean instance of a broadly useful idea.

Deterministic pattern-matching is an underrated eval layer for anything that generates customer-facing text. Instead of asking a second LLM "does this sound generic?", a cheap regex pass over your outputs catches the house-style violations and AI tells before they ship — no tokens, no latency, fully explainable. Build your own list from your brand's forbidden phrases and wire it in as a post-generation check.
