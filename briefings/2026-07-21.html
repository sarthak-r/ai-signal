The best agent runs happened while nobody was watching

Every headline item today is an agent that ran unattended. Anthropic's Levent Alpoge posted a counterexample to the 1939 Jacobian Conjecture that Claude Fable found "during the world cup final" — in degree 7, against a field that expected degree 200. A security researcher found a pre-auth WordPress RCE for about $25 by giving four concurrent agents a mandatory six-hour minimum before they were allowed to report. Linear shipped Loops, agents that fire on a schedule or an event with no human in the trigger at all. The pattern underneath all three is the same one this briefing keeps landing on: autonomy scales with verifiability. A polynomial map can be checked exactly in seconds; an exploit either pops a shell or it doesn't. Where the verifier is cheap, you can let the thing run for hours and not read the transcript. And where it isn't, the correction is already visible — Claude Code this week stopped auto-invoking its own /verify and /code-review skills, handing the trigger back to the human.

Levent Alpoge: Claude Fable produced a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecturex.com · 20 Jul

Alpoge posted an explicit polynomial map ℂ³→ℂ³ with constant Jacobian determinant −2 that is nonetheless not injective — which, if it holds, falsifies the conjecture Ott-Heinrich Keller posed in 1939. His credit line: "thanx to my close friend akhil for asking about it and my other close friend fable for working during the world cup final." The detail specialists are reacting to is the degree: the map is degree 7, where the field's working intuition put any counterexample somewhere around degree 200. The map is public and the checks are exact and independently reproducible; formal peer review and an arXiv posting have not happened yet.

Ignore the "AI does maths" framing and look at the shape of the task. This is a search over a huge space where a candidate answer can be verified exactly, cheaply, and without a human — expand the polynomial, check the determinant, find two points that collide. That is the same structure as the Bun→Rust port (a million-assertion suite) and the same structure as an exploit (it fires or it doesn't). The transferable question for your own roadmap isn't "can the model do it" but "can I check the answer for free?" Where you can, buy hours of unattended compute. Where you can't — most of financial advice — you are still paying for a human read, and no model release changes that.

Short note, load-bearing point. Willison argues coding agents have moved the break-even on undocumented-API work: "The effort to get a simple automation working has dropped, as has the cost of trying and failing to get it to work." The second clause is the real one — the blocker on integrating a closed device or platform was never only the build, it was the open-ended maintenance liability you accepted by starting. If re-deriving the integration is a cheap afternoon, breakage stops being a commitment.

Re-run your integration backlog against this. Every custodian feed, platform export, and adviser-software API you scoped and shelved because the payoff didn't justify a permanent maintenance owner now has different numbers — the ROI didn't change, the downside of it breaking did. The pragmatic move is to reclassify those from "integrations" to disposable adapters: no long-term ownership, regenerate when the vendor shifts. Worth an hour re-scoring the list; some of it is now cheap enough to just do.

This is the line being crossed right now across the app layer: from agent-you-invoke to agent-that-wakes-itself. Note what Linear had to ship alongside it — not a better model, a governance surface. Shared instructions, inspectable config, per-run audit history. That is the honest cost of removing the human from the trigger, and it's the part most teams building internal agents skip. If you're putting any agent on a cron in a regulated shop, build the audit trail before the scheduler; Linear's shape is a free reference design for what a reviewer will ask you for.

Two releases worth reading as one. v2.1.215 (19 Jul) is a one-line behavioural change: Claude no longer runs the /verify and /code-review skills automatically — they are explicit-invocation only. v2.1.216 (20 Jul) adds a filesystem isolation toggle and fixes a quadratic message-normalisation slowdown that was causing multi-second stalls and slow resumes in long sessions, plus file-op, git-worktree and background-session fixes.

A vendor deliberately reducing its agent's autonomy is rarer than the reverse and tells you something. Auto-running a review skill sounds strictly good until you notice what it does to trust: verification that fires on its own becomes ambient noise the user learns to scroll past, and it burns tokens on turns that didn't need it. Making the check explicit makes it mean something again. Same principle as today's Voices item from the other end — if the verifier isn't cheap and automatic, don't pretend it is; put a human hand on it. Also: audit your own agent UX for checks that fire so often nobody reads them.

Vercel Workflows: pin agent run-state to a home regionvercel.com · 20 Jul

Each run's state, queue dispatch, and output streams now live in a single home region held for the lifetime of the run — either where execution started or a region you name via a region option on start(). Existing workflows pick up regional placement on their next run with no code change; regional failover during incidents is included. Needs workflow@5.0.0-beta.33 or later. The changelog frames it explicitly for agents, and picks the example that matters here: "an agent serving someone in Sydney executes, checkpoints its progress, and streams output from Sydney."

Agent checkpoints are client data — mid-run state carries whatever the agent has read, which for us means holdings, balances, and personal circumstances sitting in a durable store you may never have located on a map. Data-residency reviews under the Privacy Act and CPS 230 ask where the inference runs and forget to ask where the loop's memory lives. This makes that answerable in one line of config. Whatever orchestrator you're on, go find out where your agent run-state is persisted today — the answer is often a US region nobody chose.

Build Patterns

A harness that buys results by forcing the agent to keep going.

The $25 zero-day: forcing long-horizon search with a minimum research windowslcyber.io · 20 Jul

Adam Kues adapted OpenAI's Cycle Double Cover mathematical prompt into a security-research harness and pointed it at WordPress source code rather than historical diffs. The setup is the interesting part: up to four concurrent agents, each assigned a different attack surface (input parsing, charsets, file uploads, error handling, builtin routes, serialization), and a mandatory six-hour minimum research window before any agent was permitted to conclude. Result: a pre-auth chain in software running on a claimed ~500M installs, about 10 hours to a working exploit, for roughly $25 — half a week of a $200 subscription.

Two things to steal, neither of them about security. First: the minimum-duration floor. Agents converge early and report a tidy non-answer; forcing a wall-clock or step budget before the agent is allowed to conclude is a cheap harness change that turns shallow search into deep search. Second: partition the surface across parallel agents instead of asking one agent to be thorough — thoroughness is a property of the decomposition, not the prompt. Apply both to reconciliation sweeps, document-extraction audits, or eval-failure mining. The defensive read is free and urgent: $25 and ten hours is now the cost of a competent adversary reading your dependency tree — which also means it's the cost of you reading it first.

You're not migrating; you're measuring the gap, so that when a residency question lands you have a number instead of a plan.
